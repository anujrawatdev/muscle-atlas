What They Do

PRIMARY FUNCTIONS

🏋️

FLEXION & EXTENSION

Arms · Legs

The biceps and triceps control elbow flexion and extension. Quadriceps extend the knee while hamstrings flex it — the fundamental push-pull of every limb movement.

↕️

ABDUCTION & ADDUCTION

Shoulders · Back

Medial deltoid raises the arm laterally; latissimus dorsi pulls it back to the body. These opposing forces create the full arc of shoulder mobility.

💪

PUSHING & PRESSING

Chest · Shoulders

The pectoralis major is the prime mover in horizontal pressing. Combined with the anterior deltoid and triceps, it generates all everyday pushing force.

🔄

ROTATION & STABILITY

Back · Core

The trapezius stabilizes and rotates the scapula while the rotator cuff maintains glenohumeral integrity during dynamic arm movements.

🦵

LOCOMOTION & POWER

Legs · Glutes

Gluteus maximus generates explosive hip extension. Quadriceps are critical for running, jumping, and squatting — the body's primary power output engines.

⚡

CORE TRANSFER

Core · Abs

The transverse abdominis acts as a natural lifting belt. The obliques generate rotational force and transfer power between upper and lower body.

Performance Guidance

TRAINING TIPS

01

PROGRESSIVE OVERLOAD

Muscles grow only when consistently challenged. Increase weight, reps, or volume week over week to stimulate continuous adaptation. Even a small 2.5% increase is enough signal for growth.

02

COMPOUND MOVEMENTS FIRST

Prioritize multi-joint exercises — squats, deadlifts, bench press, pull-ups — before isolation work. They produce the highest hormonal response and systemic training effect.

03

MIND-MUSCLE CONNECTION

Actively contracting the target muscle during each rep increases activation by 20–30% per EMG research. Slow the eccentric, feel the stretch, and focus on the working muscle.

04

BALANCED ANTAGONISTS

Pair opposing muscle groups: chest with back, biceps with triceps, quads with hamstrings. Imbalances cause joint stress and increase injury risk dramatically over time.

05

PROTEIN & RECOVERY

Muscle synthesis occurs during rest, not exercise. Allow 48 hours minimum between training the same group. Consume 1.6–2.2g protein/kg bodyweight daily for optimal repair.

06

FULL RANGE OF MOTION

Training through complete ranges builds strength across the entire length-tension curve, improves flexibility, and reduces injury risk — especially at the shoulder and knee joints.

07

DELOAD WEEKS

Every 4–6 weeks reduce volume by 40–50%. Deloads restore CNS capacity and allow connective tissue to recover — consistently trained athletes who deload outperform those who never stop.

08

SLEEP & GROWTH HORMONE

80% of growth hormone is released during deep sleep. 7–9 hours nightly is non-negotiable for muscle growth. Sleep deprivation alone can cut strength gains by up to 30%.
